Purpose
Why This Document Exists
Tom's name is the brand. Every output we produce either strengthens that or dilutes it.

Greenbelt Event Rentals doesn't compete on inventory or price — it competes on Tom. His name appears in every single five-star review. His responsiveness, his transparency, his genuine care — those are the product. The marketing job is not to build a brand from scratch. It is to extend Tom at scale without losing him in the process.

This document ensures that every email sequence, every caption, every subject line, every campaign piece sounds like it came from the same person — because it should. When someone reads a Greenbelt email, they should feel like Tom wrote it that morning.

The Core Test

Before publishing anything, ask one question: Could Tom have written this himself? If the answer is no — if it sounds like an agency, a template, or a marketing department — rewrite it.

Identity
What Greenbelt Actually Is
Not what the website says. What it actually is in the market.

Greenbelt Event Rentals is a small, owner-operated event rental company based in Austin, Texas. Full service — tents, tables, chairs, linens, dance floors, arches, restroom trailers. They serve weddings, corporate events, birthday parties, backyard soirées, quinceañeras, and everything in between. Their service radius is 2 hours from Austin.

The business was founded on the premise that you should deal with the owner, not a sales rep. Transparent pricing. No hidden fees. No bait-and-switch. Same quality for a 40-person birthday as for an 80-person wedding. Tom discounts for veterans, healthcare workers, and teachers — not as a promotional tactic, but because that's who he is.

Voice
Tom's Voice — The Rules
The voice is Tom. Warm. Direct. Local. Never corporate.

Tom's reviews use the same words repeatedly: responsive, thoughtful, professional, courteous, went above and beyond, fantastic price. These aren't marketing claims — they are what real clients said about a real person. The voice system is built from that reality.

01
First person singular. Emails and social posts sign as "Tom" — not "the Greenbelt team" or "we." The brand is a person, not a department.
02
Short sentences. Tom talks in plain language. No run-ons. No jargon. If a sentence needs a comma and two more clauses to make its point, break it into two sentences.
03
Confidence without pressure. Tom knows his product is good. He doesn't need to push. Offer availability. State what's available. Let the client decide.
04
Specificity over superlatives. "$799 for 40 guests, tent, tables, and chairs" beats "affordable packages for any budget." Real numbers build trust. Vague language erodes it.
05
Austin local energy. Relaxed, grounded, warm. Not hyped. Not tech-startup casual. The energy of someone who's been doing this for years and genuinely loves it.
06
No pressure tactics. Never: "limited availability," "book now before it's gone," "last chance." Tom holds dates with a conversation. That's the offer. Use it.
07
Acknowledge the human moment. Weddings, birthdays, graduations — these matter to people. Write like you know that. Not sentimentally. Just with awareness.
Voice
In Practice — Yes / No
How the voice rules apply in actual copy.
✓ This
"Hi Sarah, just wanted to check in — your event is coming up in about two months. If you're planning something again this year, I'd love to help. Same setup as last time, or something different — just reach out."
Personal. Direct. Low-pressure. Leaves the door open without demanding a response.
✗ Not this
"Don't miss out! Book your event rental package today before our peak season slots fill up. Limited availability — act now to secure your date!"
Pressure tactics are off-brand and undermine trust. Tom never talks like this.
✓ This
"Our 60-person package is $1,199 — that gets you the tent, six round tables, and 60 chairs. Delivery starts at $125 for Austin. Let me know what you need and I'll put together an exact quote."
Concrete, transparent, inviting. Tom's pricing philosophy in one paragraph.
✗ Not this
"We offer competitive, affordable pricing with premium quality rentals designed to elevate your special occasion and create unforgettable memories for you and your guests."
Generic. Says nothing. Could be any rental company in any city.
✓ This
"Just wanted to say Happy Anniversary. One year since the wedding — I hope the day was everything you'd dreamed, and the year since has been even better."
Simple, human, no ask. This is the warmth that makes Tom different.
✗ Not this
"Congratulations on your anniversary milestone! We hope your special day was amazing and that you had an incredible experience with Greenbelt Event Rentals™."
Corporate, hollow, performative. The exclamation marks don't add warmth — they replace it.
Visual Identity
Photography Direction
What the images should feel like, not just look like.
✓
Golden hour outdoor light. Warm, natural, early morning or late afternoon. Tents with soft light filtering through. Tables set for real events, not staged sterility.
✓
People in the frame. Candid moments — guests laughing, couples at tables, families at backyard events. The equipment serves the moment, not the other way around.
✓
Austin landscape context. Hill Country views, oak trees, residential yards. This is a local business. The geography should be visible.
✓
Details that show quality. Chair legs, linen texture, clean glassware, tent stakes in grass. Pride of craft is visible in close-ups.
✗
No stock photos of generic event spaces. Nothing that looks like it could be anywhere in America.
✗
No overly posed or artificially lit product shots. Greenbelt is warm and natural — the images should be too.
Channels
Rules by Channel
The voice is consistent. The format adapts.
Email
First person from Tom. 3–5 short paragraphs maximum. One CTA per email. Subject lines are conversational questions or statements — never clickbait. Signs "— Tom."
Instagram
Captions in Tom's voice — short, event-specific, location-aware. Tag Austin venues and vendors. Behind-the-scenes of setup is the strongest content format. Always include the phone number in the bio.
Google Ads
Lead with specificity: package names, pricing, guest count. "40-person tent rental Austin — $799" outperforms generic claims every time. Destination: contact page, not homepage.
Website Copy
Transparent pricing on every page. Owner contact promise on every page. No corporate language. The phrase "you will be in contact with the owner" should appear or be implied everywhere.
Review Response
Tom responds personally. Never templated. References the specific event or client interaction if possible. Thanks them by name. Short. Genuine.
Quality Control
Pre-Publish Checklist
Run every output through this before it goes live.
○
The core test: Could Tom have written this himself?
○
Does it contain at least one specific, concrete detail (price, number, location, product name)?
○
Is it free of urgency tactics, superlatives, and marketing filler language?
○
Does it use first-person singular where appropriate?
○
Are the colors, fonts, and visual elements consistent with the palette above?
○
Is it mobile-readable? Short paragraphs, no horizontal overflow, tap-friendly CTAs?
○
Does it serve the client's moment — not just the business's agenda?
○
Has Tom reviewed and approved the copy before it sends or publishes?
Non-Negotiable

No output goes live without Tom's approval on the first pass. Once the voice is calibrated and he confirms the tone is right, subsequent pieces can use this document as the standard. But the first version of everything goes through him.

This document is the starting point for every piece of Greenbelt marketing. The ICP, the email sequences, the tone guide, and the roadmap are all built on top of this foundation.
