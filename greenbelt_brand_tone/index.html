03 — Copy

Brand Tone &
Copy Framework

Four tone axes. Writing rules by channel. The vocabulary that keeps every output sounding like one person — Tom.
Foundation
The Voice Is Tom
Not "Greenbelt Event Rentals." Tom. Every piece of copy should read like something he could have written himself.

Tom's competitive advantage is not his inventory — it is his personality in contact with customers. Every five-star review names him specifically. The brand asset is a person, and the copy system exists to extend that person at scale without diluting him.

When a wedding client who booked two years ago receives an anniversary email, it should feel like Tom remembered the date and personally reached out. The fact that an automation sent it is irrelevant — if it feels like Tom, it works like Tom.

The Single Rule

Before publishing anything: could Tom have written this himself? Not "is this professional." Not "is this marketing best practice." Could Tom — warm, direct, Austin-local, slightly informal, genuinely proud of his work — have sent this? If no, rewrite it.

Tone System
The Four Axes
Every piece of Greenbelt copy sits on these four spectrums. Know where you are on each one before you write.
Axis 01
Personal → Not Corporate
Tom writes from himself, to a person. Not from a company, to a customer. First-person singular throughout. Signs as "Tom" — not "the Greenbelt team," not "we at Greenbelt Event Rentals," not "our team." The brand IS the person.
Axis 02
Specific → Not Vague
Real numbers. Real package names. Real delivery windows. "$799 for 40 guests, tent, tables, and chairs" beats "affordable pricing for every occasion" every single time. Vague language signals that you're hiding something. Tom hides nothing — and his copy should prove that.
Axis 03
Confident → Not Pushy
Tom knows his product is good. He doesn't need pressure tactics. No urgency manufacturing. No "limited availability." No "act now." The offer is available, the quality is real, the pricing is transparent — those facts do the selling. Tom's job is to make them available, not to pressure anyone into them.
Axis 04
Warm → Not Sentimental
Tom acknowledges the human moment — a wedding, a birthday, an anniversary — without performing emotion about it. The warmth comes from remembering the person, not from decorating the message with feelings. "Happy Anniversary" lands harder than "We hope your special day was an absolutely magical experience." Less is more.
In Practice
Yes / No Examples
The tone axes applied to real copy situations.
✓ Write This
"Hi Sarah, just wanted to check in — your event anniversary is coming up in about two months. If you're planning something again this year, I'd love to help. Same setup as last time, or something different — just reach out."
Personal opener, specific reference, low pressure, clear invitation, signs implicitly as Tom.
✗ Not This
"Don't miss out! Secure your event rental package today before peak season availability is gone! Limited slots remaining — book now to guarantee your date!"
Manufactured urgency. Pressure tactics. Reads like every other marketing email. Tom would never say this.
✓ Write This
"Our 60-person package is $1,199 — tent, six round tables, 60 chairs. Delivery starts at $125 for Austin. Let me know your date and what you need and I'll put together an exact quote."
Specific numbers, specific items, clear next step, no filler language.
✗ Not This
"We offer premium, competitively priced event rental packages designed to elevate your special occasion and create unforgettable memories for you and your loved ones."
Could be any rental company in any city. Zero specificity. Says nothing Tom actually believes.
✓ Write This
"Last note from me — I don't want to crowd your inbox. Whenever you're ready: (512) 710-8412 or greenbelteventrentals.com/contact. Hope to work with you someday."
Confident enough to close without desperation. Leaves the door open without groveling. The tone of someone who knows their value.
✗ Not This
"We truly appreciate your interest in Greenbelt Event Rentals and would be honored to be considered for your upcoming event! Please don't hesitate to reach out at any time!"
Overly deferential. Exclamation marks replace genuine warmth. The tone of a company that doesn't believe in its own product.
✓ Write This
"Just wanted to say Happy Anniversary. One year since the wedding — I hope the day was everything you'd dreamed, and the year since has been even better. It was genuinely an honor to be part of your day."
Simple. Real. No ask. The warmth comes from the specificity — "one year since the wedding" — not from decorative sentiment.
✗ Not This
"Congratulations on your amazing anniversary milestone! 🎊 We hope you had an absolutely incredible and magical celebration! Wishing you all the happiness in the world!!"
Emoji overload. Superlatives replace emotion. Every sentence ends in an exclamation. It performs warmth instead of expressing it.

Channels
Rules by Channel
Same voice. Different format. Each channel has its own constraints.
Email — Automated Sequences
  • Subject lines: Conversational questions or plain statements. Never clickbait. "Your anniversary is coming up — thinking of celebrating?" not "HUGE news about your upcoming event 🎉"
  • Length: 3–5 short paragraphs. People read on phones. If it can't be read in 45 seconds, it's too long.
  • CTA: One button, one ask per email. "Get a Quote" or "Check Availability" — never three competing links.
  • Signature: Always "— Tom" with phone number. Never "The Greenbelt Team."
  • Tone match: Wedding emails warmer. Corporate emails more direct. Personal/birthday emails most casual.
Instagram / Social
  • Caption length: Short-to-medium. Lead with the event, not the product. "Last Saturday in Round Rock — a backyard that became something special." Not "Check out our premium tent rental package!"
  • Content priority: Setup photos and event results over product glamour shots. Real events convert. Staged inventory does not.
  • Hashtags: Local-first. #AustinWedding #AustinEvents #BackyardWedding #CentralTexasEvents #AustinTentRental
  • Bio: Phone number must be visible. Direct contact is the entire value proposition.
Google Ads
  • Headlines: Lead with specificity. "Austin Tent Rental — From $799" beats "Best Tent Rentals in Austin" every time.
  • Description: Include the owner-contact promise. "Deal directly with the owner. Transparent pricing. Same-day quotes."
  • Landing page: Send to the contact page, not the homepage. The goal is a quote request, not a browse session.
  • Keywords: "tent rental Austin" · "backyard wedding rentals Austin" · "party tent rental Austin TX" · "event rental Austin 40 person"
Website Copy
  • Every page: The phrase "you will be in contact with the owner" or its equivalent should appear or be implied on every page of the site.
  • Pricing pages: Show exact package prices. The transparency is the conversion mechanism.
  • Contact page: The discount note for veterans, healthcare workers, and teachers should live on the contact page — it's the last thing they read before reaching out.
  • Tone: Same as email — warm but direct, specific but not formal. The website speaks for Tom when he's not available to speak for himself.
Review Responses
  • Never templated: Every response should reference something specific about the event or the client's experience if possible.
  • Short and real: Two to four sentences. "Thank you, [Name] — we loved setting up for your daughter's quinceañera. The setup crew said it was a beautiful evening. Looking forward to working with your family again."
  • Never defensive: If a negative review exists, acknowledge it, take responsibility for what's fair, and offer to make it right offline.
